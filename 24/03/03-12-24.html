by Tami Takada

From the Hyakunin isshu (百人一首), a collection of 100 poems from ancient Japan:

しのぶれど　色にいでにけり　わが恋（こい）は　物や思うと　人の問（と）うまで (Taira no Kanemori, 平兼盛)
Rough translation: “It seems my love for you is too strong to hide, for everyone asks who it is that I love”

恋（こい）すちょう　わが名はまだき　たちにけり　人知れずこそ　思いそめしか (Mibu no Tadami, 壬生忠見)
Rough translation: “Rumors of my love for you circulate, even though I believed I had hidden it well”

In the late Heian period, these two poems were composed for a poetry competition in Emperor Murakami’s court. Murakami was said to have been humming Kanemori’s poem, leading Kanemori to be deemed the winner of the competition. Mibu’s disappointment with the result was rumored to have been the cause of his death later on.

Source: Tadao Shimazu, Shinpan hyakunin isshu (Japan: Kadokawa Shoten, 1973).

by Bora Shin

We often misapply the term "外向的 [gaikouteki]" to describe social butterflies. However, this usage is inaccurate. "外向的" actually refers to "one's orientation of attention being towards external matters (興味や関心など、気持ちが自分以外の物事に向かう様子)," rather than describing someone as socially confident and outgoing. To accurately convey the idea of being outgoing and sociable in Japanese, the term "社交的 [shakouteki]" would be more appropriate.
「外向的で好奇心旺盛な人」
"A person who seeks attention outside of oneself and is brimming with curiosity"
「彼はいつも人々の中心にいる社交的な人だ。」
“He is a sociable person who always is in the center of the group.”
