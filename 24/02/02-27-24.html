by Tami Takada

Around the middle of the third century, large burial sites known as kofun (古墳) began appearing throughout Western Japan. Kofun were constructed in a variety of shapes and sizes, but the largest ones tended to be the key-hole shaped zenpōkōenfun (前方後円墳). The above picture shows Emperor Nintoku’s burial site, the Daisen-Kofun (大仙陵古墳), which is the largest known kofun with a length of 486m. Many kofun were also decorated with other items such as haniwa figures or moats.

Source: Haruo Sasayama, et al., Shōsetsu Nihonshi Kaiteiban (Tokyo: Yamakawa Shuppansha, 2022), 23-25.

by Bora Shin

In what context do you use the expression “気が置けない[kiga-okenai]”? Although “気が置けない” is often confused as “not being able to let one’s guard down (油断できない[yudan-dekinai]),” thus “needs to be careful,” it has a different meaning. “気が置けない” actually means the opposite: that there is “no need to be careful/concerned about,” or “easy to get along with (気遣いする必要がない[kidukai-suru-shitsuyou-ga-nai]).” Another expression with the Chinese character “気” in it, “気が許せない[kiga-yurusenai],” is used to refer to the state of “not being able to let one’s guard down.”
「彼女とは小学校の時からの付き合いで気が置けない仲である。」
“I have known her since elementary school, so I feel comfortable around her”

by Sara Goto

[tägäme̞]—Lethocerus deyrollei, a giant water bug that is one of Japan’s largest Heteroptera. Derived from the term “田にいるカメムシ” (the Heteroptera in the paddy field) → “田カメ” ([tä käme̞]) → タガメ.
「タガメおいしいもぐもぐ」
“Tasty Lethocerus deyrollei yum yum”
「昨日タガメに親食われたわ……マジ萎え」
“Yesterday a Lethocerus deyrollei ate my parents… Kinda bummed”
